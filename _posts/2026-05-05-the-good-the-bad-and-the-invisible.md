## The Good

AI smart glasses can make digital assistance more natural and context-aware. Unlike phones or laptops, smart glasses can provide information directly in the user’s field of view. This can help users navigate unfamiliar environments, translate signs or conversations, recognize objects, receive reminders, and access information without constantly looking down at a screen.

From an HCI perspective, the main benefit is that interaction becomes more hands-free and less disruptive. The technology has potential to support accessibility, especially for users with visual, hearing, or mobility-related difficulties. For example, AI smart glasses could describe surroundings, identify people or objects, or provide real-time captions.

AI smart glasses also show the promise of making technology more integrated into everyday life. Instead of requiring the user to stop their current activity and interact with a separate device, the system can support the user while they continue moving, working, or communicating.

---

## The Bad

At the same time, AI smart glasses create serious usability and ethical concerns. Since the device is worn on the face and can include cameras, microphones, sensors, and AI recognition systems, it may make people around the user feel watched or recorded.

Another issue is over-reliance. If users begin depending on AI smart glasses for navigation, memory, social interaction, or decision-making, they may gradually lose confidence in doing these tasks independently. There is also a risk of distraction if notifications or visual overlays interrupt the user at the wrong moment.

From an HCI perspective, the device needs to be carefully designed so that it supports the user without overwhelming them. If the interface constantly displays information, alerts, or suggestions, it may reduce attention instead of improving it.

---

## The Invisible

The most important issue is not always visible to the user. AI smart glasses may collect large amounts of data in the background, including images, voices, locations, habits, and social interactions. Even when the device looks simple from the outside, the system behind it may involve complex data processing, cloud storage, facial recognition, personalization, and algorithmic decision-making.

This creates an “invisible” layer of interaction. Users may not fully understand what data is being collected, how it is processed, who can access it, or how long it is stored. People nearby may have even less control, because they may be captured by the device without directly agreeing to use the system.

This is especially important because AI smart glasses do not only affect the person wearing them. They also affect bystanders, classmates, friends, strangers, and people in public spaces. This makes privacy and consent more complicated than with normal personal devices.

---

## My Learning Process

Through preparing the video presentation, I learned that the promise of a technology should be evaluated together with its hidden consequences. At first, AI smart glasses seem exciting because they offer convenience, accessibility, and real-time assistance. However, when viewed through an HCI perspective, the design problem becomes more complex.

I learned to ask questions such as:

- Who is the user?
- Who else is affected by the device?
- What data is being collected?
- Does the user understand what the system is doing?
- Can the user control or correct the system?
- How can the system make invisible processes more transparent?

This helped me understand that good interaction design is not only about making a device easy to use. It is also about making the system understandable, responsible, and socially acceptable.

---

## My Responsibility and Achievement

For this presentation task, my responsibility was to analyze AI smart glasses from both a positive and critical perspective. I focused on presenting the benefits of the technology while also identifying the risks that may not be obvious at first.

My main achievement was organizing the discussion into three parts:

- **The Good**: benefits such as accessibility, convenience, and hands-free interaction.
- **The Bad**: risks such as distraction, over-reliance, and social discomfort.
- **The Invisible**: hidden issues such as data collection, privacy, consent, and background AI processing.

This structure helped me explain the technology in a balanced way and connect it to HCI concerns.

---

## Personal Reflection

This task helped me understand that emerging technologies should not be judged only by how advanced or useful they appear. AI smart glasses may provide powerful support, but they also introduce new challenges because they are wearable, always present, and able to sense the surrounding environment.

The most important lesson I learned is that invisible system behavior can be one of the biggest problems in HCI. If users do not understand what a system is doing, they may lose trust or be unable to make informed decisions. For AI smart glasses, this issue becomes even more serious because the device can collect information not only about the user, but also about other people nearby.

In future design work, I would pay more attention to transparency, consent, and user control. A responsible AI smart glasses system should clearly show when it is recording, explain how data is used, allow users to control privacy settings, and avoid unnecessary distraction.

Overall, the presentation helped me see AI smart glasses not only as a futuristic product, but as a complex interaction system involving users, bystanders, data, trust, and social behavior.
